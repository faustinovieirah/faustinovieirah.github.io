About me

Email: helena.faustinovieira@astro.su.se

Olá! I am Helena Faustino Vieira, originally from Câmara de Lobos in Madeira, Portugal. I moved to the UK in 2016 for my Master's degree in Physics, Astrophysics and Cosmology, where I've graduated with distinction. During my undergraduate career I dabbled in several research areas, such as observations of exoplanet transits and the kinematic alignment (or misalignment) of hot gas and stars in quenched galaxies (as a potential tracer of past mergers).

I received my PhD at Cardiff University in 2024, under Ana Duarte-Cabral and Tim Davis. My thesis consisted of analysing the star-forming molecular gas in nearby galaxies, through a novel dust extinction mapping technique. For more details, please check my research and publications sections.

Research

Research interests

Some of the outstanding big questions in star formation and ISM my research is focused on are: understanding what sets the structure and properties of the interstellar medium (ISM) within a galaxy, and also understanding the role of galactic environment on star formation. I am particularly interested in disentangling the effects of galactic shear and stellar feedback on the regulation of star formation. Since these phenomena operate on very different timescales, determining which is dominant (and where in the galaxy) has significant implications on the lifetimes of molecular clouds, and consequently the time-frame under which star formation occurs.

High-resolution dust extinction mapping technique

In order to understand the link between galactic environment and star formation, we need observations that can resolve individual star forming regions as well as the disc structure of galaxies. Throughout my PhD, I have developed a dust extinction technique which is able to image the dust (and gas) in nearby spiral galaxies at a parsec-scale resolution, which resulted in my first publication. As a proof-of-concept, I have applied the method to M51 - a nearby, grand-design spiral galaxy - and obtained a gas surface density map of its entire disc with a resolution of 5 pc (or 0.14”), shown below (from Faustino Vieira et al., 2023).

Parsec-scale molecular clouds in M51

Our extinction technique allows for detailed statistical studies of individual cloud complexes, and is therefore a useful tool to study the structure and characteristics of the ISM as a function of galactic environment and dynamics.

We obtained over 13,000 resolved molecular clouds in our high-fidelity sample from our map of M51 (catalogue available below), and performed an in-depth analysis of trends between cloud properties and large-scale environment, as well as galactocentric distance (Faustino Vieira et al., 2024). We find that galactic environment and dynamics (in particular the interaction between M51 and its companion galaxy) have a significant impact on molecular cloud properties.

Projects

FFOGG (Following the Flow of Gas in Galaxies)

I am part of the core team of the FFOGG project, which is focused on understanding the physical processes dictating the flow of gas in galaxies from the diffuse, large-scale interstellar medium (ISM) to dense star-forming regions. We want to investigate how stars form within the galactic context, and establish a link between the large-scale environment and the small-scale physics of star formation. For that purpose, we naturally need information that spans many orders of magnitude in terms of physical scale and densities.

The FFOGG project aims to do just that: understand how spiral galaxies perform as star formation engines. To do this, we link both high-resolution observations and sophisticated simulations of the Milky Way and nearby spiral galaxies. I am leading the nearby galaxy observational side of this project. Please visit the FFOGG website for more details on our work, and for a list of related publications and available data products.

Outreach

I have always had a passion for making scientific research fun and accessible to a wider audience. During my undergraduate, I worked as an outreach demonstrator giving engagement talks in local schools as well as participating in large-scale events such as Stargazing Live at Porstmouth Historic Dockyard, which gathered over 5000 people in fun space-themed activities and talks aimed at all ages. I have carried this work into my PhD, co-organising and hosting Cardiff's first ever Astronomy on Tap events, which aim to bring entertaining and educational talks by professional Astronomers to the general public in a casual setting.
